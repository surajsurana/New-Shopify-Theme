01 — Understanding K&A

The Corset, Explained

What a K&A corset actually is, and how construction — not just cut — is what sets it apart.

A corset lehenga pairs a structured, boned bodice with a lehenga skirt — it's a different construction from a traditional lehenga blouse, not just a styling variation of one. A conventional blouse is cut and stitched to follow the body; a corset is built with boning and structured lining to hold its own shape and, in doing so, shape the wearer — defining the waist, supporting the bust and rib cage, and holding its line through a full evening of standing, sitting, and dancing.

For a closer look at what separates the two, see K&A's Journal piece, The Corset Lehenga, Reconsidered: Structure as Bridal Statement.

Structure is a construction method, not a look. A genuinely structured corset uses boning (rigid or semi-rigid strips built into internal channels), reinforced lining, and a cut drafted specifically to shape the waist, bust, and rib cage — so the bodice holds its own shape independent of the body inside it. A corset-style blouse can borrow the visual cues (a fitted bodice, a laced front) without any of that internal architecture, which is why it can look structured in a photograph but behave like ordinary fitted fabric the moment you move in it.

K&A applies this same construction principle — boning, lining, defined cut — across corset lehengas, corset drape sarees, and corset-anchored Indo-Western sets. Full detail in What Makes a Corset "Structured": Construction, Fit, and the K&A Approach.

The corset construction itself doesn't change between the two — boning, lining, and cut work the same way in both. What changes is what the corset is paired with: a corset lehenga sets the structured bodice above a fitted-waist, often flared or fishtail skirt with its own separate construction, while a corset drape saree sets it above a pre-draped saree silhouette, where the corset frequently anchors the drape itself rather than relying on pleating and tucking alone. Neither is more structured than the other — it comes down to whether you want a lehenga's fuller, more traditional bridal silhouette or a drape saree's leaner, more contemporary line.

02 — Fit & Occasion

Sizing & Occasion

Getting the fit right, and finding the piece for your moment — bridal or otherwise.

K&A works from a standard XS–XL size chart (bust, waist, and hip measurements) as a starting point, and offers personalised consultations and bespoke fitting appointments at the Santacruz, Mumbai studio — or via video consultation for clients who can't visit in person, including international clients. Because a structured corset does real shaping work — rather than just following your measurements the way a soft blouse does — fit matters more here, not less; working through it directly in a consult is a more reliable way to get the bodice right than sizing from measurements alone.

Get in touch to book a consult.

K&A's range covers the full arc of a wedding calendar — bridal wear for the wedding day itself, and pieces built for sangeet, mehendi, reception, and cocktail moments, in both fuller bridal silhouettes (lehengas) and leaner, more contemporary ones (drape sarees, Indo-Western sets).

What a piece costs, how long it takes, and what's possible to change along the way.

K&A's pieces range from ₹30,000 to ₹280,000+, depending on the piece, embellishment density, and construction complexity. This is a considered, high-involvement purchase rather than an entry-level one — K&A doesn't run discount or sale pricing.

K&A pieces are made-to-measure, so standard returns and exchanges generally aren't offered. This makes getting sizing and fit right upfront — via a consult, in person or by video — especially worthwhile.
